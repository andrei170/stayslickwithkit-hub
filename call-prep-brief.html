Open the Call With

Keep it warm. Pre-frame as a working session, not a pitch. Three minutes of context before any tools come out.

"Kit, before we dive in, I want to be useful, not just talk. I've spent a few hours pulling apart your funnel front to back. I've got a clear read on three things that I think are leaking real money, plus a question or two I couldn't answer from the outside. Mind if I run them past you and then we can decide what's actually worth doing?"

That gives him the floor when you ask the questions. Listen.

Three Open Questions to Resolve First (Before Pitching)

These are the things I couldn't verify from public data. Get the answers before you open with the recommendations — half the pitch lands harder once these are confirmed.

  1. "Walk me through the offer stack quickly. Is the $27-50 community on Skool or somewhere else?" I couldn't find a public Skool. The members area runs on Squarespace. If there's no community yet, that's a build opportunity. If it's gated/private, fine.

  2. "When someone fills out the application form for the 1:1, what happens next? Does it auto-redirect to a calendar, or does someone reach out manually?" This is THE question for the cancellation issue. If it's manual, you've found the leak in 60 seconds. If it auto-books, then the leak is pre-call nurture.

  3. "Are you running any paid traffic right now? Meta, YouTube, anywhere?" Public signals say no, but check. If yes, you can offer to audit. If no, that's a separate $5-10K/mo lever after the funnel is plumbed.

Bonus probe if it fits: "Who's helping you on the back end right now — a VA, a partner, an agency?" Tells you whether you're replacing someone or building the first version.

Your Three Recommendations — Validated, Sharpened, Sequenced

#1 — VSL Funnel for the $4K (your strongest lead — open here)

The framing he won't have heard before:

"Kit, here's the bit that surprised me. You've got a VSL on /cym-vsl for the course. You don't have one for the 1:1. Your $500 product is more funnel-equipped than your $4,000 product. That's backwards."

Why this lands: It's specific, it's observable, it flips a flaw he probably feels into a one-line story. He already believes in VSLs (he built one for the course). You're not selling him on the format. You're pointing at where the format is missing.

The mechanic: - Short VSL (3-5 min) on a dedicated /1-1-vsl page - Page hits before the application form, not after - VSL ends with the form embedded directly below (no second page jump) - Application form auto-redirects to Calendly the second it submits - Confirmation email + 1 reminder + day-of SMS to crush no-shows

Estimated impact: Even held-call rate going from 5/9 to 7/9 + close rate of 1-of-3 instead of 1-of-5 = adds $4-8K/mo before any other change.

Reference: "Sabrina Zohar runs a tighter funnel at the $349 tier than you do at $4K. Stephan Speaks closes 3-session $1,197 packages on a single sales page. The format works at every price point. You're just not running it where it pays the most."

#2 — Sales Process / DM-Setting (4 of 9 cancellations is a CALENDAR problem, not a setter problem)

Reframe the failure for him:

"4 of 9 isn't a setter problem. It's a calendar problem. The form submits and dies — that's where the warm money is leaving. By the time anyone reaches out manually, half the people have cooled off, gone on holiday, or remembered they were going to ghost you anyway."

The fix has three layers:

  1. Form → Calendar in one step. Application Typeform/form ends with: "Pick a time below" — embedded Calendly or Cal.com. No human in the loop until they show.
  2. Pre-call nurture sequence. 3-touch sequence between booking and call: confirmation video (60-90s), case study email (T-1 day), morning-of SMS. 65%+ show rate floor.
  3. Setter playbook (if he keeps a setter). Standardised opener, 3 qualifying questions, no pitching in the DM, single job is to confirm fit and protect the calendar slot. See dm-setting-playbook.

Estimated impact: Cancel rate 44% → 15-20% gets him to 7-8 held calls of 9 booked. Same close rate, ~50% more revenue per month from the same top-of-funnel.

#3 — Content Angle for Wealthier Demos (the bridge between volume and the $4K)

The honest read:

"Your top-performing reels are 'why he ghosted me' and 'why he won't text back.' That content pulls 28-year-old women who've been single 6 months. Your offer is 'find a high-quality man, fast.' The hook tier and the offer tier don't match — and that's why the calls aren't wealthy enough."

What changes (without burning his viral engine):

  • Keep the punchy provocative hooks for top-of-funnel reach. They work.
  • Add a second content track specifically targeted at 30-45 professional women: founders, lawyers, doctors, ex-corporate.
  • Hook examples (see content-strategy for full list):
  • "Why high-earning women keep dating men who can't keep up"
  • "How to date when your career is bigger than the men you meet"
  • "I help women who run companies date with the same standards"
  • Podcast guest pivot. Stop interviewing same-tier creators. Start booking female founders, female VCs, c-suite women. They become content AND become buyers. Both at once.
  • Photoshoot upgrade. He's photogenic and theatre-trained. A luxury-feel content shoot (Mayfair restaurant, suit, tailored aesthetic) reskins the brand for wealthier buyers in a weekend.
  • Skip the "Hitch" framing. It's young/cute. Replace with "I work with women whose dating lives don't match the rest of their lives."

Estimated impact: This is the slow-burn lever. 60-90 day pickup. Doesn't fix this month's revenue. Compounds the next quarter. Frame as "the thing that keeps the funnel filled with $4K buyers, not $50 members."

Sequencing the Pitch (Don't Front-Load Everything)

If he's a good listener, lead with #1. It's the fastest revenue lever and the easiest yes. Don't lead with #2 — pitching a sales process fix sounds like criticism. Pitching a missing VSL sounds like discovery.

Order: 1. Open the VSL gap (1 → "your $500 has a VSL, your $4K doesn't") 2. Wait for him to nod 3. Connect it to the cancellation issue (2 → "and once that VSL is in place, here's how we plug the calendar leak") 4. Land the content shift (3 → "and here's the 90-day version of the brand reskin so the calls coming in are wealthier")

Three angles, one logical chain. Each one earns the next.

Likely Objections and How to Answer

Objection What you say
"I'm still acting, I don't have time for a project" "That's why you'd hire us. We install. You keep filming. The whole point is you don't manage it."
"I tried a VSL before and it didn't move the needle" "Where was it placed? Was it on the $500 page or the $4K page? And what was the form behaviour after?" — you'll find the same thing. Most VSLs fail because the page they live on isn't built for them.

"Can I think about it?" "Course. Quick thing — what would you need to see to feel good about it?" Get the real objection, not the polite no.

What NOT to Do on the Call

  • Don't show all three recommendations as a flat list. Sequence them.
  • Don't pitch price. Defer to a follow-up proposal. Don't be pulled into pricing on a 3-way call — it makes the relationship transactional too early.
  • Don't tell him he's been doing it wrong. Tell him what's missing. "You've got X, you don't have Y" beats "you should be doing Y."
  • Don't promise specific revenue numbers. Use language like "in our experience" / "the math typically lands at." Revenue estimates are for your prep doc, not for him.
  • Don't reference the Trustpilot count or his small customer base. That's an internal note. Telling him "you've only got 17 reviews" is insulting and doesn't help the pitch.
  • Don't reference "Featured in BBC" being weak. That's an internal observation about his bio framing. Save it for the build phase, not the pitch call.
  • Never mention payment terms. Wire/Whop only, but not on a cold-warm call. Surfaces after he says yes to the proposal.
