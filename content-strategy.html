Phase 3

Content Strategy — Pivoting to Wealthier Demos

The brief: keep the viral engine, add a second content track that pulls 30-45 professional women with budget. 90-day plan, hook bank, podcast pivot, photoshoot brief.

The Problem in One Line

Kit's audience is buying "why won't he text me back." His offer is "find a high-quality man fast." Different people. The hook tier and the offer tier don't match.

The Fix in One Line

Run two content tracks in parallel. Keep the viral track for top-of-funnel reach. Add a wealth-track for $4K conversion.

The Two-Track Model

Track A — Viral / Top of Funnel (KEEP)

Audience: Women 22-32, single 6-18 months, app-fatigued, frustrated with situationships. Function: Volume reach. Drives membership and course buyers. Format: Punchy provocative reels. 30-45s. Strong text overlay. Ghosting / unavailability / commitment hooks. Cadence: 3-4 reels per week. (No change to current cadence.) Examples that already work: - "Real Reason They Ghosted" - "Men Age Like Fine Wine" (34.5K TikTok likes) - "If He Wanted To, He Would" - "Why He's Emotionally Unavailable"

This track funds the rest. Don't kill it. Don't dilute it.

Track B — Wealth / High-Ticket Pull (NEW)

Audience: Women 30-45, career-built (founders, lawyers, doctors, ex-corporate, senior IC), single 12+ months by choice or circumstance. Function: Pulls $4K 1:1 buyers. Lower volume, higher conversion-per-view. Format: Slightly longer (45-90s), calmer pacing, less stunt-like text. Kit speaking directly to the camera in a more curated environment. Cadence: 2-3 pieces per week, mixed reel + carousel + podcast clip. Why it works: Wealthy professional women don't engage with hooks aimed at 24-year-olds. They scroll past "he ghosted me" content. They stop on content that names their actual experience.

Hook Bank for Track B (Wealth)

These are tested-pattern hooks rebuilt for the 30-45 professional woman. Use them as starting points, vary, A/B.

Identity hooks (pull her in by naming her)

  1. "If you run a company and your dating life looks like a mess, this is for you."
  2. "How to date when your career is bigger than the men around you."
  3. "Why high-earning women keep dating men who can't keep up financially."
  4. "The dating advice you've found online isn't built for the woman you've become."
  5. "You don't need 'just put yourself out there.' You need a screen that actually filters."
  6. "The 30-45 professional woman dating problem nobody is talking about."
  7. "I work with women who run businesses. Their dating problem isn't lack of options. It's lack of standards that match the rest of their life."

Pattern interrupt hooks (challenge a belief)

  1. "'Be more vulnerable' is the worst dating advice you can give a high-performing woman. Here's why."
  2. "Your career didn't make you intimidating. It made you visible. There's a difference."
  3. "You're not picky. You're calibrated. Stop apologising for it."
  4. "The men telling you to 'soften' your standards are the ones who couldn't meet them."
  5. "Most dating advice for women is built for the woman who hasn't built her life yet. You're past that."

Story / mechanism hooks

  1. "How one of my clients went from three years single while running a business to engaged in eight months — without lowering a single standard."
  2. "I worked with a surgeon last year. Three weeks in, she'd already cut her dating chaos by 60% — without going on a single new date."
  3. "The 10-minute fit check that takes the guesswork out of a first date."
  4. "If you're not running men through a screening process, you're auditioning for them."
  5. "What changes when a woman with a career stops dating like a 22-year-old: the men get serious within weeks."

Provocative / cinematic hooks

  1. "The men dating up are the ones who'll go the distance. The ones dating across? Watch out."
  2. "If a man doesn't like the most successful version of you, he wasn't dating you in the first place."
  3. "Most men can't keep up. The ones who can — won't tell you. They'll show you. That's the screen."

Counter-positioning hooks (against entry-level dating advice)

  1. "Stop reading dating advice written for women in their twenties. You're playing a different game."
  2. "Tinder isn't broken. It's just not built for someone like you. Here's what is."
  3. "'High-value man' content is for entertainment. Real dating standards are for life. Don't mix them up."

Podcast Pivot

Current state

Kit's podcast ("Stay Slick with Kit") is heavy on same-tier creators. Recent guests: "Tamara," "Mel," "MrMoneyJar," "Yasmine." Format works, conversation is good — but the guests pull a same-tier audience.

What changes

Pivot 60-70% of new bookings toward female founders, female VCs, c-suite women, surgeons, lawyers, journalists, broadcasters.

These guests do three things at once: - Pull their own audience (often more affluent than the existing one) - Become content (long-form value, evergreen) - Become buyers themselves. A 38-year-old founder coming on his podcast as a guest is a higher-probability $4K buyer than 100 random viewers.

Outreach criteria for new guests

  • Female, 30-45
  • Career-built (visible role, real audience or real org)
  • 10K-150K social followers (sweet spot — big enough to bring an audience, not so big they have agents/PR teams)
  • Vocal about dating / relationships either humorously or seriously (so the conversation has substance)
  • UK or US bases preferred (timezone, legal jurisdiction for high-ticket buyers)

Sample target list (build this out — 50 names)

  • Female founders 30-45 in London who post about dating
  • Hosts of women-targeted podcasts
  • Authors of recent dating books for adults (not 20-somethings)
  • Lifestyle / career creators with female-skewed audiences

Episode positioning

Instead of "let's talk about dating," frame as:

  • "What it's like to date when you're running a company"
  • "The dating standards I've earned vs. the standards I was told to have"
  • "Why successful women are choosing to be alone — and when that's a mistake"
  • "Reset your dating life in your thirties"

Each episode title is also a Track B hook.

Photoshoot Brief

Most overlooked lever. Kit is photogenic and theatre-trained. His current visuals undersell him.

Why this matters

Wealthy buyers buy aesthetic before they buy substance. The first impression of his brand right now (homepage, IG grid) is "talented coach with cleanish branding." It needs to be "this is the man I'd hire."

One-day shoot, 4-6 looks

Look Setting Wardrobe Use
Editorial portrait Mayfair restaurant interior, soft window light Tailored suit, no tie, white shirt, polished shoes Hero image on landing page, podcast cover, press
Lifestyle Walking through a quiet Mayfair / Marylebone street, golden hour Casual blazer, fitted dark jeans, loafers IG grid, Track B reel B-roll
Studio Charcoal seamless backdrop, dramatic lighting Charcoal turtleneck or black T under blazer Headshot, About page
Interview Library / hotel lobby, leather chair, warm lamps Sport jacket, open collar, watch visible VSL set, podcast video
Couple coaching Same Mayfair restaurant, with two female "client stand-ins" (or two real clients with permission) at a table Same as look 1 Site testimonial section, social proof
Action / candid At his desk with laptop, real client call simulated Same as look 4 Trust signal images on landing page

Photographer brief

  • Editorial style. Think Esquire or British GQ, not Instagram-influencer.
  • Available natural light + minimal flash. No harsh studio lighting.
  • 50mm or 85mm prime. Shallow depth.
  • Deliver 30-50 finals (RAW + edited), 8K thumbnails for video, 16:9 + 9:16 + 4:5 crops.
  • Budget: £1,500-3,000 for a London-based photographer with editorial portfolio.

Filmed B-roll while you're at it

Same day, hire a videographer for 4 hours. Capture cinematic B-roll for Track B reels: - Walking, looking out a window, writing, sipping coffee, mid-conversation, reading a book - Standard 4K, slow-motion option, log profile for grading

That B-roll powers 6-12 weeks of Track B content without needing him in front of the camera each time.

Brand Tightening (Adjacent Lever)

Bio rewrite

Current: "Giving you a PhD in Men. Leading Professional Women 25+ to High Quality Relationships. Featured in BBC."

Issues: - "PhD in Men" is cute but young - "Featured in BBC" is ambiguous and stretches an acting credit - Doesn't speak to the wealthy demo

Proposed:

"Working privately with successful women to find men who actually match them. Theatre-trained. London-based. 12-week 1:1. Apply ↓"

Or:

"Helping career-built women date as well as they live. 12-week 1:1 coaching, by application. ↓ Start here."

Pick one, A/B for two weeks.

Drop the "Hitch" framing

It's young. It's cute. It's also tied to a 2005 Will Smith film. The wealthy 38-year-old founder doesn't want to be coached by Hitch. She wants the discreet, considered, expert version.

Replace internal positioning with:

"I work with women whose dating lives don't match the rest of their lives."

That's the line. Use it everywhere — VSL, bio, About page, press intros.

90-Day Implementation Plan

Weeks 1-2: Setup

  • Photoshoot booked + executed
  • 4-hour B-roll filmed same day
  • 30 wealth-track hooks scripted from the bank above
  • 5 podcast guest outreach emails sent
  • Bio updated
  • "Hitch" framing removed

Weeks 3-4: Production

  • 8 Track B reels filmed and edited
  • 2 Track B carousels designed
  • 2 podcast guest interviews booked + recorded
  • Track B reels start posting

Weeks 5-8: Iteration

  • Track engagement on Track B vs. Track A
  • Identify top 3 Track B hooks that hit
  • Double down on those formats
  • Continue podcast guest pipeline

Weeks 9-12: Compound

  • Track B should be 30-40% of total content output by now
  • Demo of new application form-fillers should start shifting
  • First wealth-demo $4K closes from new content track

Month 4+ ceiling

  • Track B carries 50% of output
  • New buyer profile is 30-45 professional, not 22-32 frustrated
  • Average buyer LTV up 30-50%
  • $4K close rate up because pre-sold demo is qualified before they ever land on the page

What NOT to Do

  • Don't kill Track A. The viral engine pays the bills. Wealth-track compounds slower.
  • Don't try to make every reel both viral AND wealth-track. They're different formats. Don't blend.
  • Don't move the brand colour palette / aesthetic chasing wealth. Same brand, sharper visuals.
  • Don't pretend to be more affluent than he is. The whole pitch is authenticity. The shift is in WHO he's talking TO, not who he claims to be.
